# How to Improve Your Volley in Padel: A Complete Net Guide

The volley is the most important shot in padel. Period. No debate. If you control the net, you control the match. And if your volley is weak, you're losing points that should be yours.

The good news is that the volley can be improved with technique and practice. You don't need to be a pro, you just need to understand what you're doing wrong and fix it step by step. In this guide, we'll cover everything you need to know to transform your volley into a lethal weapon.

## 1. Net position: the magic triangle

The ideal net position forms a triangle between you, your partner, and the contact point. Stay close to the net (about 2-3 meters), with your knees bent and your weight forward. If you're too far back, you give the opponents time to react. If you're too far forward, they'll lob over your head and you're exposed.

The trick: feel like you're about to fall forward. That tension keeps you alert and ready to move in any direction. Your feet should never be still: make constant micro-movements, as if you were dancing on your toes.

Communication with your partner is key at the net. Before each point, agree on who covers which zone. Generally, the player on the ball side takes the volley, but that varies depending on the situation. Call out "mine" or "yours" loudly to avoid confusion. A communication error at the net is a gifted point.

## 2. Preparation: less is more

The most common mistake in the volley is preparing like you're hitting a drive. The volley doesn't need brute force, it needs control and precision. The simpler your preparation, the more consistent you'll be.

Keep the paddle up, at chest or shoulder height. The elbow should be relaxed, not glued to your body but not extended either. Wrist firm but not tense. The backswing should be short: take the paddle back only 20-30 centimeters. There's no time for long preparations at the net.

The grip is fundamental. Use a continental grip for both forehand and backhand volleys. This position lets you switch quickly between sides without moving your hand. If you have to change your grip for each volley, you'll always be late.

## 3. Contact point: forward and up

The ideal contact point is in front of your body, at shoulder or chest height. If you hit the ball at waist height, you're too low and giving the opponent an advantage. If you let it pass behind your shoulder line, you lose power and accuracy.

The paddle should make contact with the ball slightly open to generate spin. A small downward slice makes the ball pick up speed after the bounce and becomes much harder for the opponent. But be careful: don't scoop it, just a slight downward angle.

If the ball gets past your shoulder line, don't force the volley. It's better to let it go and play defensive than to gift a point with a forced volley. Patience is a virtue in padel, especially at the net.

## 4. Spin: the secret key

A flat volley is predictable. The opponent sees it coming and can return it. A volley with spin is unpredictable and much harder to control. Learn to apply topspin (bottom to top) so the ball picks up speed after the bounce.

Spin also lets you play with more safety. A volley with backspin (slice) is easier to control than a flat one at the same speed. The ball with slice lands dead at the opponent's feet, forcing them to play an ascending shot from below.

Experiment with different spins during practice. The topspin volley is ideal for passing over the opponent when they're close to the net. The slice volley works better for dropping at their feet. The flat volley with side spin is perfect for taking the opponent off the court.

## 5. Types of volleys and when to use them

The cross-court volley is the hardest but the most effective. When you master the cross-court volley, you can pass between both opponents or find the sideline. It's especially useful when the opponent on the opposite side is too far forward.

The down-the-line volley is safer but less rewarding. Use it when you're uncomfortable or when the opponent on your side is poorly positioned. It's the conservative option that keeps the point alive.

The body volley is strategic. When one of the opponents is unprotected, aim directly at their torso. It's hard to return and generates forced errors. But don't overuse it: if you always go for it, they'll anticipate.

The volley at the feet is nearly impossible to return well. When the opponent is very far back or jumping, aim at their shoes. A ball that arrives low and fast is almost impossible to attack.

## 6. Winning mentality at the net

The net is a territory of risk and reward. If you're afraid, you fail. If you step in with confidence, you dominate. The right mindset is: "this volley is mine." Hesitation means losing.

But confidence doesn't mean recklessness. Choose wisely which balls to volley and which to let pass. A good volley at the right moment wins the point. A bad forced volley loses it. Patience and aggression must coexist.
